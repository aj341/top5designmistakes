Mistake #1

The 'Canva Trap' for Premium Content

Using DIY tools for high-stakes documents is costing you credibility with every proposal you send. Click to read more.

⚠ The Problem

Using Canva for high-stakes documents — proposals, annual reports, pitch decks — signals that your firm is not investing in its own presentation. While Canva is excellent for social media, it was never built for professional-grade deliverables at the level sophisticated clients expect.

📈 The Business Impact

Proposals look templated and generic. Sophisticated clients notice. You may win the brief on paper but lose the room because your materials do not match the premium price tag you are charging.

✅ How to fix it

Reserve Canva for quick social assets. Route all proposals, decks, and reports through a professional design workflow with proper brand templates, typography hierarchy, and production-quality output. The investment pays for itself on the first deal it helps close.

Mistake #2

Inconsistent Brand Voice on LinkedIn

Your company page looks professional. Your people's profiles are a fragmented mess diluting your authority every day.

⚠ The Problem

Individual partners and employees post content with varied, off-brand visuals. Different banner images, inconsistent headshot styles, and mismatched post graphics across personal profiles present a fragmented picture to anyone researching your firm.

📈 The Business Impact

LinkedIn is where B2B trust is built. If your firm's people look visually disconnected, it raises doubts about whether you operate as a cohesive unit. Prospects build trust through personal profiles — and that is where your brand is breaking down.

✅ How to fix it

Create a LinkedIn brand kit: standardised banner templates, headshot editing guidelines, and post graphic templates for your team. Make it easy for every person to represent the firm consistently without needing a design degree to do it.

Mistake #3

Lack of Sales Enablement Polish

Your sales team is walking into final pitches with outdated decks and generic one-pagers. You are losing deals at the last hurdle.

⚠ The Problem

Sales teams are using outdated or poorly designed PowerPoint decks, case study PDFs, and one-pagers. These bottom-of-funnel assets are where deals are won or lost — yet they receive the least design attention, left to whoever has time between meetings.

📈 The Business Impact

A strong service offering presented in a weak deck makes the whole firm look less capable. At the final pitch stage, your competitor with the polished presentation has a measurable advantage. You have done all the work to get in the room and then let design undermine you.

✅ How to fix it

Treat your sales collateral as a living asset that gets refreshed quarterly. Every pitch deck, case study, and capability statement should be production-quality and on-brand. Build a system where updating them is frictionless — not a 3-day project every time a pitch comes in.

Mistake #4

Generic Website & Landing Page Visuals

Your website looks identical to every competitor. You have 8 seconds to differentiate — and you are wasting them.

⚠ The Problem

Relying on generic, overused stock photography that does not reflect your firm's unique value or the Australian market. The "handshake in a glass office" image says nothing about you specifically. When your website looks like every competitor, prospects have no visual reason to remember you.

📈 The Business Impact

High bounce rates, low engagement, and prospects who cannot articulate what makes you different when comparing providers. You are failing to build the emotional connection that makes firms memorable — and memorable firms win more inbound referrals.

✅ How to fix it

Commission custom illustrations, iconography, or photography that reflects your specific expertise and Australian context. Even a refreshed visual language — custom icons, a distinct colour application, branded graphic elements — dramatically separates you from the pack without a full rebrand.

Mistake #5

Neglecting Internal Branding

Plain internal documents are a missed opportunity to reinforce culture — and a signal to your team about how much you value quality.

⚠ The Problem

Training materials, onboarding guides, company announcements and internal reports are plain, unengaging, and look nothing like the polished external brand. This creates a two-tier experience: the brand you sell externally, and the reality employees experience internally.

📈 The Business Impact

Poor internal design leads to lower engagement with critical materials, weaker culture reinforcement, and higher friction in onboarding. Employees who do not feel proud of how their firm presents itself internally are less likely to advocate for the brand externally.

✅ How to fix it

Apply your brand standards to internal communications with the same rigour as external ones. Branded onboarding decks, formatted training guides, and designed internal newsletters signal to your team that quality matters at every level — building the culture you want to project externally.
